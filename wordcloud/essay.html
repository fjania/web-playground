How a Word Cloud Actually Works

The algorithms behind fitting words into shapes.

What makes a good word cloud?

Most word clouds are boring. You have seen them: words floating in a rectangle, evenly spaced, surrounded by dead space. Each word sits inside an invisible bounding box that keeps everything politely distant. The result looks like a parking lot — orderly, utilitarian, forgettable.

The interesting word clouds are the ones where words nestle into each other's curves, where the letter y dips into the gap above a lowercase a, where the whole composition feels dense and intentional. Achieving that requires solving a surprisingly rich problem: how do you pack hundreds of words at wildly different sizes without any overlap, fast enough that a user can watch it happen?

The answer involves bitmaps, bitwise arithmetic, Archimedean spirals, and some clever geometry. This essay walks through each piece.

From text to bitmap

This is what the algorithm sees when it looks at a word.

Each colored cell is one bit, 32 bits packed into a single integer — one row of the word's shape stored as a handful of numbers. A single bitwise AND can test 32 pixels for overlap simultaneously.

We render onto an OffscreenCanvas rather than a visible one because the rasterization is a throwaway step — we only need the pixel data, and OffscreenCanvas avoids polluting the DOM or triggering reflows. We use textBaseline = 'alphabetic' because it gives a consistent anchor across fonts; other baselines shift depending on ascender/descender metrics, which would misalign the bitmap relative to the placement coordinates.

Why pack 32 pixels into a single integer? The next section makes that clear.

Words can also be placed at 90° or 270° rotation. Rotating a word changes the shape of its bitmap entirely — a wide, short word becomes tall and narrow — which alters how tightly it can pack against its neighbors and often improves density in regions where vertical gaps dominate.

The collision problem

These two words need to share the canvas. How do we check if they overlap? Drag the second word around to find out.

The actual implementation handles the case where a sprite is not aligned to a 32-pixel boundary by bit-shifting. If a sprite starts at pixel position 17, its first 32-bit word needs to be split across two board words using right-shift and left-shift operations. The code tests (bits >>> rShift) & board[idx] for the first part and (bits << lShift) & board[idx + 1] for the spillover. This is the trickiest part of the implementation, but it means we never sacrifice accuracy for speed.

The packing is straightforward. For each row, we iterate through the pixels left to right. Pixel x lands in integer x >> 5 (integer division by 32) at bit position 31 - (x & 31) (the remainder, counted from the most significant bit). A set bit means occupied. The result is a compact binary mask of the word's shape, typically just a few hundred bytes for even large words.

Searching for a home

A 1000×600 canvas has 600,000 pixel positions. We cannot test them all. Scrub through the spiral to see how the algorithm narrows the search.

The classic approach is an Archimedean spiral. Starting from the placement origin (usually the center of the canvas), a point traces an outward spiral, and at each position along the spiral we test whether the word fits. The beauty of the spiral is that it searches nearby positions first, then gradually expands. This means words cluster tightly near the origin, creating dense packing.

The spiral's angular increment determines the spacing between successive test positions: too large and you skip over valid gaps, too small and the search takes forever. For each word, the spiral search terminates the moment it finds a position where the bit-packed collision test returns clear. If it exhausts its step budget (30,000 steps in this implementation) without finding a position, the word is skipped. In practice, the first few large words place quickly near the center, while later small words may need to search longer to find gaps — but the fast collision test keeps each step cheap.

Reading the distribution

Before choosing a scaling function, it helps to understand the shape of your data. A sparkline — a tiny chart of word frequencies, sorted from highest to lowest — immediately reveals the distribution type.

A steep cliff that drops off fast is a power-law distribution: a few words dominate, and most words are rare. Square root or log scaling will keep the smaller words visible. A gradual slope is a more even distribution: linear scaling works fine. A nearly flat line means all words have similar frequencies — the cloud will look uniform regardless of scaling, so layout choice matters more than sizing.

The full word cloud tool displays this sparkline in its description bar, alongside the min and max frequencies and the total word count. This metadata is not decoration — it is the information you need to make informed choices about how to render the cloud.

Sizing and scaling

A word's frequency in the source text determines its size in the cloud. But the mapping from frequency to font size is not obvious. If the most common word appears 500 times and the tenth most common appears 50, should the first word be 10x larger? That would make it dominate the entire canvas while everything else is tiny.

Three scaling functions offer different tradeoffs. Linear scaling maps frequency directly to font size — a word with twice the count is twice as large. This works well when word frequencies are relatively uniform, but with real-world text (which typically follows a power-law distribution), the top word dwarfs everything else. Square root scaling compresses the range: a word with 4x the count is only 2x as large. This is the default and works well for most data. Logarithmic scaling compresses even further, making the size differences between common and rare words quite subtle.

The right choice depends on your data's distribution. A nearly uniform distribution (all words have similar counts) can handle linear scaling. A steep power-law distribution (a few words dominate) needs sqrt or log scaling to keep the smaller words visible.

Color, font, and spacing

The algorithmic core — rasterization, collision detection, spiral search, layout — determines where each word goes. But the aesthetic impression comes from the surface choices: color palette, typeface, and padding.

Color palettes assign colors to words in sequence, cycling through 8 hues. The palette choice dramatically changes the mood — vibrant neons versus muted earth tones versus monochrome. Padding, set via the stroke width during rasterization, controls how tightly words pack. Zero padding means words can touch (sometimes overlap at the anti-aliased edges). High padding creates visible gaps. And the font changes everything about the shapes of the letter silhouettes, which in turn changes how tightly the collision detection can pack them.

The details matter. The bit-packing makes collision detection 30x faster. The spiral search produces dense, natural-looking packing. The layout algorithms give each cloud a distinctive silhouette. And the scaling functions ensure your data's story comes through in the relative sizes.
