Pattern: Brick
Rewrite note. An earlier version of this page walked through a shiftAlternate-based recipe. That recipe produces offset stripes, not a canonical brick pattern. Real brick-pattern boards use a uniform primary species with thin mortar strips, an asymmetric compose (half-brick at one end) to create the row offset, flipAlternate rather than shift, and SpacerInsert to add the horizontal mortar between rows. This page has been rewritten accordingly.

1. Recipe & target

From the v2.1 design doc's corrected Brick pattern card:

Compose(M, w, M, w, ..., M/2) → Cut(rip=0) → Arrange + Preset(flipAlternate) + SpacerInsert × (N-1).

Three ideas work together:

  • Asymmetric compose. The panel's first and last pieces are a full-width maple and a HALF-width maple. Between every pair of maples is a thin walnut strip (the vertical mortar). This asymmetry is what produces the row offset after flipping.
  • flipAlternate preset. Every odd-index slice is rotated 180° about its vertical axis. That mirrors its x-composition — the half-maple that was at the right end is now at the left end, and the mortar positions land between where the even-row mortars landed. Result: the walnut mortar lines run at half-brick offsets between even and odd rows.
  • SpacerInsert × (N−1). Between every pair of arranged slices, a thin walnut strip is inserted. This is the horizontal mortar. Without it, slices abut directly and the row boundaries are barely visible glue joints, not prominent mortar lines.

Concrete numbers used in this walk-through (cleanly in units of 10 mm for readable JSON and SVG):

  • Maple brick: 50 mm wide.
  • Walnut mortar (vertical, composed into panel): 10 mm wide.
  • Half-brick (last strip): 25 mm wide.
  • Strip height (y): 50 mm. Strip length (z): 400 mm.
  • Crosscut pitch: 50 mm → 8 slices, each 205×50×50.
  • Horizontal mortar (SpacerInsert, walnut): 5 mm thick along z. Inserted between every pair of slices → 7 spacers total.

Final output panel bbox: 205×50×435 (400 from slices + 35 from 7 spacers × 5 mm). The Z-axis extends by 35 mm.

2. Step 0 — default state (same as checkerboard)

Bootstrap — default 3-feature timeline loaded

User action: opens /end-grain/3d-cut.html. defaultTimeline() instantiates the usual 3 features (ComposeStrips with 2 strips, Cut, Arrange). Identity arrange renders the starting panel back to its source form.
This step is identical to checkerboard Step 0. 2-strip maple/walnut panel, crosscut at pitch=50 → 8 slices, identity arrange = reassembled back to original. No PlaceEdits, no Presets, no SpacerInserts. The real work starts when the user edits the compose step in Step 1. Full JSON on the checkerboard page if you want it.

3. Step 1 — user builds the brick-style compose

Asymmetric compose: maple + walnut + ... + half-maple

Clicks the species-catalog + button alternately (+ maple, + walnut, + maple, + walnut, + maple, + walnut) to populate seven strips. For the final strip, sets the per-species width to 25 mm and clicks + maple to append the half-brick. Pipeline regens on each click.

Observations

  • Strips can have different widths. Up to now every walk-through used uniform widths. Here widths vary per strip (50 / 10 / 25). The compose step already supports this — it's just the first walk-through that exercises it.
  • The half-brick (strip-6, width 25) is the offset mechanism. It's a plain StripDef; nothing special about it geometrically. What's special is its position — being the last strip means it ends up at the opposite x-extreme after flipping.
  • Slice provenance is identical across all 8 slices because rip=0 makes every crosscut span the full strip set. Same property as checkerboard.
  • Panel bbox after identity arrange matches the input: 205×50×400. Nothing interesting yet — that comes in Step 2 and Step 3.

Observations

  • Offset emerges from asymmetric compose + flip. Without any shift operation, the half-maple on one end and full-maples on the other produce a natural ½-brick offset between even and odd rows. This is the algorithmically elegant trick that makes brick work in an end-grain model.
  • Output bbox is identical to input bbox. Rotation preserves the panel's extent — unlike shift, which would widen it. Good sanity check that the v2.1 design's "rotate preserves bbox" claim holds for this slice geometry.
  • Walnut mortar positions in even rows: x = 5, 11, 17 (three vertical strips).
    Walnut mortar positions in odd rows: x = 2.5, 8.5, 14.5 (three vertical strips, offset by 2.5 mm from even rows — exactly half of the 50 mm brick width).
  • But the pattern doesn't read as "bricks" yet. Slices abut directly along z — the only horizontal lines between rows are glue joints (thin, barely visible). The brick-wall look needs the horizontal mortar that comes in Step 3.
  • The cut tile is unchanged. The preset targets arrange-0; nothing upstream moves. Confirms the timeline's separation of concerns.

Observations (the meaty step)

  • Z-axis bbox extends. Input was 205×50×400. Output is 205×50×435. The 7 spacers × 5 mm = 35 mm contribute directly to the panel's length. This is the first walk-through where an Arrange changes a dimension other than x.
  • Spacers aren't PlaceEdits. They don't target a slice and they don't transform one — they insert new material into the arrangement. That's why they need a distinct feature kind instead of being a PlaceEdit op.
  • Arrange's result grows a new field: appliedSpacerCount. Keeping spacer bookkeeping separate from placement-edit bookkeeping means the timeline UI at v2.13 can highlight spacers distinctly (different icon, different colour). Conflating them into appliedEditCount would lose information.
  • SpacerInsert.afterSliceIdx is a positional reference — same as a PlaceEdit's sliceIdx. It faces the same resolution problem at regen: if the upstream Cut produces fewer slices, spacers with out-of-range afterSliceIdx go to error. Same resolver path as PlaceEdits, just for a different feature kind.
  • 7 spacer features is a lot of boilerplate for a user to add one-by-one. There's a strong case for a preset-like affordance (e.g. "insert a spacer between every pair"). Discussed in §7.
  • The pattern finally reads as a brick wall. Vertical mortar from the compose, horizontal mortar from the spacers, row offset from the flip. Three independent intents, three feature kinds, one classic pattern.

6. Invariants of the whole pipeline

Tracking what each feature preserves vs. changes. Tells the pipeline tests what to assert, and tells the visualizations what to make visible.

StepPreservedChanged
ComposeStrips (step 1) n/a (creates the panel) creates a 205×50×400 panel with asymmetric compose (4 M + 3 w + 1 M/2)
Cut (default) per-slice band composition slice count (1 → 8), z-extent per slice (400 → 50)
Arrange (identity) slices abut flush along N; output bbox equals input bbox nothing (identity)
Preset(flipAlternate) (step 2) slice count, per-slice composition (as a set), output bbox (205×50×400) x-ordering of bands in odd slices (mirrored)
SpacerInsert × 7 (step 3) slice count, slice compositions, slice x-positions output bbox extends along z: 400 → 435 mm

Notable: only two features in this pipeline change the output bbox (Cut and SpacerInsert), and they change it along orthogonal axes. Preset(flipAlternate) preserves it (unlike shiftAlternate, which would extend x). This orthogonality is a sign the feature vocabulary is well-decomposed — each intent has a clear dimension it owns.

7. What this walk-through newly exposes

Adds the following to the findings stack on top of checkerboard's:

7.1 SpacerInsert needs to be declared in v2.1 types even though it ships at v2.17

The canonical brick recipe requires SpacerInsert. That means the Feature union type must include SpacerInsert from day one, with full target/params shape, even if the runtime treats it as a no-op until v2.17. Otherwise the types have to be retrofitted, and worse, any AppState loaded from a file wouldn't round-trip safely.

This already matches the v2.1 design doc's position (SpacerInsert is in the Feature union from v2.1) — confirmed here.

7.2 Arrange's result needs appliedSpacerCount + appliedSpacerSources

Parallel to the appliedEditCount/appliedEditSources fields that track placement edits, spacers need their own bookkeeping. Timeline-panel UI at v2.13 can then attribute each spacer back to its originating feature.

Implication for v2.1 types: add two fields to the Arrange result shape.

7.3 Pipeline execution order: Arrange also depends on SpacerInsert features

Building on checkerboard's §5.1, Arrange now has three classes of dependency:

  1. Its upstream Cut (for slices + provenance).
  2. Presets targeting it (for preset-expanded PlaceEdits).
  3. PlaceEdits targeting it.
  4. SpacerInsert features targeting it.

The two-phase approach still works: phase 1 indexes all four categories by arrangeId; phase 2 executes features in timeline order with Arrange consulting the index.

7.4 Asymmetric compose is geometrically cheap but changes the pattern vocabulary

Brick is the first canonical pattern that REQUIRES different widths for different strips. Checkerboard, herringbone, basket weave — all use uniform widths. Only chaos (deliberate randomness) and brick (for the half-maple) need the variance.

The compose step already supports per-strip widths (StripDef.width), so nothing changes in the model. But the UI affordance for "set this strip to half-width" needs to be ergonomic — otherwise users will get it wrong and the brick pattern won't offset correctly. Propose a per-strip width input, plus a "×½" shortcut button on each strip row.

7.5 Two axes of bbox extension now exist

Previously (before this walk-through was rewritten) I thought bbox extension was mostly about shift ops along x. Actually it's richer:

  • Cut extends one axis (creates the slice list; length becomes pitch).
  • Preset(shiftAlternate) extends x (perpendicular to cut normal). Not used for brick — still valid for other patterns.
  • SpacerInsert extends z (along cut normal). Used for any pattern with horizontal mortar.

Arrange's result shape has to accept that any axis can grow. Not a type change — Panel.bbox is already 3D — but a correctness concern for tests and for scene-layout code.

7.6 The "offset via asymmetric compose" trick is worth documenting

The cleverness of using a half-brick at one end instead of a shift op is non-obvious. A user coming from tile-CAD mental models would naturally reach for a shift. A woodworker learning the app would recognize the half-brick trick immediately. The design doc's Brick pattern card should mention this explicitly (done in the revision that accompanies this rewrite).

7.7 shiftAlternate as a preset may not be needed for any canonical pattern

Reviewing the 13 patterns in the v2.1 design doc:

  • Checkerboard: flipAlternate ✓
  • Brick: flipAlternate + SpacerInsert ✓ (not shiftAlternate!)
  • Herringbone, chevron, diamond: flipAlternate / mirrorAlternate at 45° cut
  • Basket weave, pinwheel: rotateAlternate(90) or rotate4way
  • 3D cube, quilt, pixel, chaos, ripple, Celtic knot: various — none obviously use shiftAlternate

It's possible shiftAlternate is a preset without a canonical home. Worth flagging as removable from v2.12 unless a pattern surfaces that genuinely needs it. Rough experimental patterns might still use it as a free-form tool — so leaving it in as an advanced/uncommon preset is defensible.

8. Open questions after this exercise

  • Should there be a "spacer-every-row" preset? Adding 7 SpacerInsert features by hand is tedious and error-prone. A preset that expands into N−1 SpacerInserts (for N slices) would parallel the way flipAlternate expands into N/2 PlaceEdits. Recommendation: add Preset variant spacerEveryRow with params { species, width }. Mechanically identical to the PlaceEdit-generating presets; just generates SpacerInsert features instead.
  • Does the "spacer species" default to the panel's mortar species? If the compose has walnut mortar strips and the user opens the spacer UI, the natural default is walnut. Worth a UX touch — inspect the ComposeStrips for the most common "thin" species and suggest it.
  • Per-spacer width uniformity. All 7 spacers in this walk-through are 5 mm. If a user wanted to vary widths (7 mm, 5 mm, 7 mm, …) they'd add 7 distinct SpacerInsert features. That's supported by the model as-is; no additional affordance needed. Flagged for completeness.
  • What happens when a SpacerInsert's afterSliceIdx references a slice that no longer exists? Same failure modes as PlaceEdit: error, feature skipped. But there's a subtle difference: a missing PlaceEdit just drops its transform; a missing SpacerInsert drops its material contribution. The downstream panel is visibly shorter (in z) by the spacer's width. That's a real user-visible consequence — worth surfacing in the timeline-panel status message.
  • Should the pattern card's SVG match this walk-through's exact dimensions? The design doc's Brick card uses 3-unit bricks × 5 rows for visual density, while this walk-through uses 5-unit bricks × 8 rows for didactic clarity. Different aesthetics, same pattern. Not an inconsistency, but worth keeping them recognizably similar.
  • Could the horizontal mortar come from the cut step instead? Imagine a "cut with offcuts kept" mode where the offcuts become thin walnut-stained spacers. Probably not worth exploring — the flexibility of SpacerInsert with arbitrary species/width is cleaner than coupling mortar generation to the Cut step.
